## Issue 1 - Critical Missing Assumption: Local Utility Rate Stability and Future Escalation
The plan uses centralized firing (Decision 3) to mitigate high energy costs but *assumes* DKK cost per KWh remains stable (Assumption 6). Remote Arctic utility rates are often volatile or subject to unannounced hikes. This compromises long-term profitability.

Recommendation:

- Immediately model profitability with a staggered 10% annual utility rate increase for three years.
- Negotiate fixed-rate contracts for primary electric load for Year 1/2, even at a premium.
- If fixed rates are impossible, establish a contract trigger: if energy costs exceed 30% of variable operating expenses (excl. rent), membership pricing tier (Decision 14) must trigger a mandated 5% surcharge.

Sensitivity: 15% utility rate increase in Year 2 causes 6-9% decline in gross margin per piece. Membership price hike risks 10-15% local membership churn, delaying ROI by 4-8 months.

## Issue 2 - Missing Assumption: Viability of High Off-Peak Hourly Rental Rate for Revenue Stability
The 'Builder's Balance' relies on 25% revenue from high hourly rentals (Assumption 1), acknowledged as a risk (Risk 1). The plan assumes local users (prioritizing affordability) will pay high ad-hoc rates.

Recommendation:

- Define minimum viable hourly rate (e.g., 150 DKK/hour) to cover total operational overhead per hour utilized.
- If local users refuse this rate, pivot revenue model from speculative rentals to guaranteed sources, e.g., converting non-instructor time to pre-paid, lower-margin 'local co-op time' slots (Decision 11, Choice 1).

Sensitivity: If hourly rental revenue is only 60% of target (25% of total revenue), the project runs a deficit of 150,000–250,000 DKK in Year 1, consuming nearly the entire 15% contingency fund (approx. 300,000 DKK total), jeopardizing Q4 sustainability.

## Issue 3 - Under-Explored Assumption: Local Labor Law and Contract Instructor Flexibility in Greenland
Decision 1 favors contracting instructors for scheduled dates, transferring idle time risk to them. The plan *assumes* Danish/Greenlandic labor law permits this flexibility (no guaranteed minimum hours, easy termination) without mandatory severance or establishing permanent employment.

Recommendation:

- Conduct immediate legal consultation (10,000 DKK from contingency) to verify contract model legality in Nuuk.
- If 80-day threshold mandates benefits/severance, fixed labor cost is much higher than implied by 2 FTE assumption.
- If high rigidity is confirmed, pivot to salaried model (Decision 1, Choice 2) for resilience (Risk 3 mitigation) and to avoid large payouts.

Sensitivity: If regulation mandates 4 weeks' severance or 50% compensation for idle time, fixed labor cost increases by 20-35% in Year 1, exhausting 60-90% of contingency, delaying essential infrastructure improvements.

## Review conclusion
The 'Builder's Balance' strategy provides a sound structural foundation (contingency funding, staffing). Critical structural dependencies exist outside direct project control: stable utility rates, local willingness to pay rental premiums, and favorable labor contracting laws. Financial resilience against these operating frictions is missing. Immediate action needed to legally vet the staffing model and use scenario-based cost modeling (10% utility inflation) to stress-test reliance on off-peak hourly rentals to prevent early contingency buffer depletion.
